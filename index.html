Sustainability Through the Ages

Explore Intel's journey through time, discovering how our commitment to innovation has shaped a more sustainable future for technology and our planet.

1968

Intel Founded

Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore rename the newly formed company NM Electronics to Intel Corporation, laying the foundation for decades of technological innovation.

1968: Intel Founded

Intel began in Mountain View, California, at the heart of Silicon Valley. Venture capitalist Arthur Rock helped secure early funding. The company first succeeded in semiconductor memory like SRAM and DRAM before focusing on processors. The name “Intel” comes from Integrated Electronics. From the start, the founders promoted a flat, open-office culture instead of traditional corporate hierarchy.

1971

First Microprocessor

Intel debuts the 4004, the world's first commercial microprocessor, igniting the microprocessor revolution and propelling the future of computing devices.

1971: First Microprocessor

Intel debuted the 4004 chip, which was originally commissioned as a custom component for a Japanese calculator company. Designed by a team led by Federico Faggin, it became the world's first commercially available microprocessor. By squeezing an entire central processing unit onto a single, fingernail-sized piece of silicon, it permanently changed the trajectory of computing.

1978

8086 Processor

Launch of the 8086 processor, establishing the x86 architecture that drives countless PCs and servers in the modern era.

1978: 8086 Processor

Engineered in just 18 months to face rising competition, this landmark chip introduced a powerful 16-bit architecture. IBM later selected a variant of this design to run its original Personal Computer, cementing Intel's market dominance. The processor also established the legendary x86 blueprint, introducing a standard of backward compatibility that still influences modern computing.

1985

386 Processor

Intel introduces the 386 processor with 32-bit architecture, ushering in a new era of performance and multitasking for personal computers.

1985: 386 Processor

This chip introduced 32-bit architecture and advanced hardware virtualization, allowing computers to run multiple operating systems simultaneously. Packing nearly 275,000 transistors, it gave software developers the processing power needed to build smooth, graphical user interfaces like early Windows. Intel's strategic decision to be the sole manufacturer of this processor fundamentally shifted its industry leverage.

2006

Peak GHG Emissions

This year marks Intel's highest annual greenhouse gas emissions for operations. Over subsequent years, Intel invests heavily in chemical abatement, renewable energy, and energy-efficient manufacturing to reverse this trend.

2006: Peak GHG Emissions

Rapid global manufacturing expansion caused Intel’s operational greenhouse gas emissions to hit an all-time high this year. Recognizing the environmental impact, the company immediately pivoted toward installing advanced chemical scrubbers and developing cleaner manufacturing processes. This critical turning point forced Intel to shift from localized green projects to a centralized, systemic corporate environmental policy.

2020

RISE Strategy

Intel launches its RISE (Responsible, Inclusive, Sustainable, Enabling) strategy and 2030 goals, aiming to drive industry-wide progress on climate action, water stewardship, and waste reduction.

2020: RISE Strategy

Intel launched its comprehensive RISE framework, deliberately tying executive compensation directly to achieving specific sustainability targets. The strategy outlines strict 2030 goals, including a zero-waste-to-landfill circular economy and net-positive water usage at manufacturing sites. It also focuses heavily on diversifying technical leadership and enforcing ethical standards across the global supply chain.

2022

Net-Zero By 2040

Intel announces its commitment to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions (Scope 1 and 2) across its global operations by 2040, building on years of environmental initiatives.

2022: Net-Zero By 2040

Intel committed to achieving net-zero greenhouse gas emissions across its global operations a full decade ahead of the Paris Agreement targets. The aggressive plan focuses on eliminating direct factory emissions (Scope 1) and indirect emissions from purchased energy (Scope 2). To succeed, researchers are actively inventing alternative chemical alternatives to replace high-global-warming etching gases.

2023

Renewable Electricity

The company achieves 99% renewable electricity usage worldwide, helping to drastically lower carbon emissions and driving progress toward Intel's long-term sustainability goals.

2023: Renewable Electricity

The company successfully transitioned its global operations to 99% renewable electricity by signing massive green power purchase agreements. Entire manufacturing hubs across the US, Europe, and Asia bypassed fossil-fuel grids in favor of wind, solar, and on-site green installations. This massive milestone proved to the wider tech sector that energy-intensive semiconductor fabrication can run sustainably.

2024

Sustainability Summit

Intel hosts its first Sustainability Summit, uniting suppliers, government officials, and industry leaders to collaborate on next-generation sustainable semiconductor manufacturing.

2024: Sustainability Summit

Intel hosted its inaugural Sustainability Summit, gathering suppliers, policymakers, and competitors to collaborate on eco-friendly semiconductor manufacturing. The event focused heavily on tackling Scope 3 supply chain emissions and sharing blueprints for high-volume industrial water recycling. It concluded with a joint accord among tech firms to standardize green packaging and lower logistics footprints.
